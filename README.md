# Nuclear Safety Project

An interactive website examining five major nuclear incidents from 1961 to 2011 and how modern reactor design fixed each failure mode.

Live site: jjkubiak5-netizen.github.io/Nuclear-safety

## What's On The Site

Each major nuclear incident is presented with a short summary, a diagram of what went wrong, and the key stats (INES level, deaths, radiation release). The site also covers:

- Why a "stopped" reactor still needs cooling (decay heat physics)
- Four reactor designs compared side by side
- The "defense in depth" safety layers in modern reactors
- Deaths per terawatt-hour by energy source
- How reactor risk has dropped across four generations of designs

## Built With

Plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No frameworks, no build step. Custom SVG diagrams and an animated risk chart that draws itself when you scroll to it.

## How To Run Locally

Open `index.html` in any browser. That's it.

## Sources

Incident data is cross-referenced against primary sources:
- IAEA reports (including INSAG-7 for Chernobyl)
- U.S. Nuclear Regulatory Commission documents
- The Kemeny Report (Three Mile Island)
- National Diet of Japan Fukushima report
- Our World in Data for energy comparison statistics
- Kharecha & Hansen (NASA GISS, 2013) for lives-saved estimates

## About

Built as a civic action project for 8th grade. The goal is making the case that nuclear power is statistically one of the safest energy sources we have, despite its reputation, and to encourage more honest public discussion about nuclear energy.
